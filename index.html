About me.

My name is Carl-Eric Wegner and I'm a postdoctoral researcher in the lab of Kirsten Küsel at the Friedrich Schiller University of Jena, and yes - I like microbes. Being a trained soil microbiologist and a learning-by-doing bioinformatician I consider myself as microbiology "generalist". Being broadly interested in microbial ecology and physiology, my research is overall dedicated to (complex) carbon turnover in oligotrophic (and anthropogenic) environments.

The focus of my research has been on on top-down / bottom-up microbial carbon cycling in (artificial) wetlands including for instance peat bogs and rice paddies. With respect to top down processes I'm interested in learning how microbes partake in complex carbon turnover. Which microbial groups respond to complex carbon amendment, how do they process it, who is teaming up to do the job?

In the context of bottom up carbon cycling I'm curious about microbes that are involved in metabolizing and recycling one-carbon compounds. These form for instance food webs and are important biogeochemical players on our planet as only known biological sink for compounds that are known to be strong drivers of global climate change (e.g. CH4).

More recently I started to work on an exceptional, anthropogenic microbial habitat - early industrial soft coal slags originating from mineral leaching. These slags, remotely similar to AMD systems, are acidic, particular rich in iron, aluminium, sulfur, and they do harbour exceptional, uncharacterized microbial life, adapted to this harsh, oligotrophic environment.

In order to address these research questions I combine a combination of cultivation-dependent and -independent techniques including especially (meta)genomics and (meta)transcriptomics.
